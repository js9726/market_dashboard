# Entry Methods

**Summary**: The two primary entry methods across traders in this wiki are (1) pullbacks to rising moving averages and (2) breakouts from tight consolidations — each with different risk/reward characteristics and timing tools.

**Sources**: Alex's Swing Trading System.md, The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md, The Complete Traders' Guide.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-23

---

## Alex's Approach: Pullback to 21dma (No Breakouts)

Alex's edge is explicitly **not** in breakouts. The system focuses on entering weakness as stocks pull back into the rising 21dma-structure. (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

> "No Breakouts" — Alex's Golden Rule #3

### Entry Types

| Entry Type | Condition |
|---|---|
| Weakness into 21dma | Stock pulls back to 21dma on lower volume; buy the dip |
| Reclaim & Backtest | Stock reclaimed 21dma after a break; buy the backtest from above |
| Tight Range Reversal | Tight multi-day range near 21dma; buy breakout of tight range |

All entries: price must be within **0 to 1x ATR** of 21dma structure (source: Alex's scans (TradersLab).md)

### Timing
- Execute in **first hour** or **final 30 minutes** of market session
- Use **daily charts exclusively** — don't over-optimize on intraday noise
- MCO/MCSI for broad market confirmation

---

## SRxTrades: Two Methods

### Method A — Breakout Entry

Best used when a stock has formed a **tight consolidation** (bull flag / pennant) after a big move:

1. Identify tight, coiled pattern above moving averages
2. Volume decreasing on consolidation (demand still present, supply absorbed)
3. Trigger = break above prior day's high / trendline / base resistance
4. Use **5m or 15m chart** for sniper entry at the precise breakout level
5. Stop = low of day → tiny stop, large potential trend

(source: The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md)

### Method B — Buying RS on Weakness (Moving Average Pullback)

Best used in established strong trends:

1. Stock pulls into **8/21/50 EMA** on **low volume** (institutions accumulating)
2. Avoid high-volume pullbacks (institutions distributing)
3. Look for **staircase pattern**: slow, controlled pullback in a series of slightly lower highs
4. Entry confirmation: **first 30-minute green candle** (30M pivot)
5. Entry trigger: break of the high of that 30M candle
6. Stop = low of day

Key insight: institutions watch the same EMAs → they *create* the demand at those levels, making them reliable. (source: The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md)

---

## Jeff Sun's Entry Discipline

Jeff uses **RVOL** (Relative Volume) as the primary entry confirmation tool:
- Strong RVOL on a breakout = institutional conviction; high probability follow-through
- Weak RVOL = avoid; breakout likely to fail
- **LoD < 60% ATR**: entry where the stop (low of day) is within 60% of the stock's daily ATR — keeps risk tight

(source: The Complete Traders' Guide.md)

Jeff also uses **VCP** (Volatility Contraction Pattern) as the setup precondition:
> "I will NEVER enter a stock with prior loose price action" — Jeff Sun

A VCP signals the supply/demand imbalance is resolving in favor of buyers — the pattern tightens before expansion.

## Qullamaggie: Breakouts and Episodic Pivots

Qullamaggie's long-side entries are built around fast-moving stocks with a precise risk point. The two main long setups are:

| Entry Type | Condition | Trigger | Stop |
|---|---|---|---|
| Common Breakout | Strong prior 30-100% move, then 2-week to 2-month tight consolidation with higher lows and tightening range | Break above base or opening-range high (1m/5m/60m) | Low of day, not wider than ATR/ADR |
| Episodic Pivot | Fresh catalyst, ideally 10%+ gap and huge volume near the open | Opening-range high (1m/5m/60m) | Low of day |

Quality filters:
- Prior momentum or catalyst must be obvious.
- Base should be tight, not wide and loose.
- Volume should confirm the breakout or EP.
- Entry should not be late after the move has already expanded.
- Avoid breakouts into nearby overhead supply.

See qullamaggie-breakouts-episodic-pivots for the full rubric.

## Volume at Entry

Across all traders, volume is a key confirmation:
- **Breakout** entries: need volume surge to confirm demand
- **Pullback/MA** entries: need low volume on the pullback (supply exhausted); volume can come on the bounce
- High volume *into* a pullback = potential distribution → wait or avoid

| Mistake | Problem |
|---|---|
| Chasing after breakout has extended | Terrible R/R; stop too far away |
| Buying pullback on high volume | May be distribution, not accumulation |
| Entering on market open impulsively | Wait for first hour or use 30M pivot confirmation |
| No market phase alignment | Even great setups fail in wrong market phase |

# Risk Management

**Summary**: Risk management is the set of rules and habits that prevent a bad trade from becoming a catastrophic loss, and bad luck from becoming a blown account.

**Sources**: Alex's Swing Trading System.md, Alex's Trading Psychology Reflections.md, The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md, Post by @Clement_Ang17 on X.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-23

---

## Core Philosophy

> "Defense is Offense" — Alex's Golden Rule

Controlling downside is not a constraint on returns — it is the *source* of returns. Traders who survive long enough compound; traders who blow up do not come back. (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

## Stop Losses

### Alex's Soft Stop Approach
Stops are **structure-based**, not fixed percentages. A close below the 21dma-structure signals the trade thesis has failed. (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

- Stops adjust as the stock moves: the 21dma rises → stop rises
- Do not use hard % stops (e.g. "I'll stop at -8%") — let price structure define the level

### Jeff Sun's <60% ATR LoD Rule
Entries where the stop (low of day) is within 60% of the stock's ATR allow for tight, precise risk control. This is one of Jeff's core execution disciplines. (source: The Complete Traders' Guide.md)

### SRxTrades' Low-of-Day Stop
On breakout entries, the stop is the **low of day** — creating a tight risk/reward where a small stop funds a large potential trend ride. (source: The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md)

### Qullamaggie's Low-of-Day + ATR/ADR Guardrail
For Qullamaggie-style breakouts and episodic pivots, the initial stop is normally the **low of day**. The stop should not be wider than ATR or ADR; if the stop is wider than the stock's normal daily range, the setup usually loses its asymmetric risk/reward. (source: Qullamaggie official setup posts)

## Trimming / Scaling Out

Systematic partial exits protect gains while keeping exposure for the trend:

**Alex's approach:**
- Trim **1/3** at 2R (locks in early profit, finances the trade)
- Hold final **2/3** using 21dma-structure as trailing stop

**SRxTrades' 4-tranche approach:**
- 25% at major resistance
- 25% on first close below 8 EMA
- 25% on 21 EMA break
- 25% on 50 EMA break on closing basis

(see position-sizing for full detail)

## Exposure Management by Market Phase

| Phase | Max Exposure |
|---|---|
| Early recovery / out of correction | 10–20% of risk budget |
| Confirmed uptrend | Build to full exposure |
| Extended / overbought | Stop adding, trim strength |
| Correction / breakdown | Cut to cash |

(source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

## Qullamaggie Account Risk Limits

| Risk Control | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Account risk per trade | Usually 0.25-1.0%; rarely more than 1% |
| Typical position size | 10-20% of account |
| Single-name overnight cap | Avoid holding more than 30% of account overnight in one stock/ETF |
| Stop basis | Low of day for breakouts/EPs |
| Liquidity | Skip or reduce size if the stock is too illiquid for the intended position |

## Earnings Risk

- **Never hold through earnings** unless position has enough cushion to absorb the implied move
- Cushion rule: unrealized gain must exceed the implied earnings move before risking a hold
- Most positions: exit before earnings (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

## Mental Capital is a Resource

Risk management extends beyond money. Overtrading in choppy markets drains **mental capital (MC)** — the cognitive and emotional reserves needed to execute well. Protecting MC is part of risk management. (source: Alex's Trading Psychology Reflections.md)

## Common Risk Failures

| Mistake | Consequence |
|---|---|
| Widening stops after loss | Doubles the loss; breaks system |
| Averaging into a loser | Turns a manageable loss into a disaster |
| Holding through earnings without cushion | Binary wipe-out risk |
| Overtrading after a loss | Trader death spiral |
| Ignoring market phase | Good setups fail in bad markets |

(sources: Alex's Trading Psychology Reflections.md, Post by @Clement_Ang17 on X.md)

# Position Sizing

**Summary**: Risk-based position sizing is how traders control downside on each trade. Multiple sources converge on scaling in gradually rather than going full-size at entry.

**Sources**: Alex's Swing Trading System.md, The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md, The Complete Traders' Guide.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-23

---

## Alex's Risk-Based Framework

Alex sizes positions as a percentage of **account equity risked per trade** — not a fixed share count or dollar amount. (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

| Conviction Level | Risk % per Trade |
|---|---|
| Weakness entry (less confirmation) | 0.25% of equity |
| Confirmed entry | 0.5% of equity |
| High-conviction setup | Up to 1% of equity |

**Initial allocation**: 10–20% of total risk budget per name at first entry. The remainder is added on through **scaling in** as the trade proves itself.

## Building a Position (Scale-In)

1. Start with 10–20% of intended full position
2. Add 2–3 times as confidence grows and price confirms
3. Maximum 3 adds; each add requires the stock to hold structure

This approach creates a **cushion** — early profits on the first tranche fund the risk on subsequent adds. Cushion acts as leverage without margin. (source: Alex's Swing Trading System.md)

## SRxTrades' Trimming Framework

@SRxTrades uses a 4-tranche **trim-as-you-go** approach to exit winners (source: The blueprint to consistently making $10km+ in trading.md):

| Tranche | Size | Exit Trigger |
|---|---|---|
| First trim | 25% | Sell into strength at big resistance |
| Second trim | 25% | First close below 8 EMA |
| Third trim | 25% | Break of 21 EMA |
| Final trim | 25% | Break of 50 EMA on closing basis |

This keeps exposure running for the full trend while systematically locking in gains.

## Jeff Sun's ATR%-Based Sizing

Jeff Sun uses **ADR%** (Average Daily Range) to calibrate position size — higher ADR% stocks get smaller allocations because their larger daily swings translate to bigger dollar P&L swings at the same share count. (source: The Complete Traders' Guide.md)

## Qullamaggie's Risk + Position Cap

Qullamaggie sizes from account risk first, then caps by exposure and liquidity. Most trades risk **0.25-1.0%** of account equity, typical positions are **10-20%** of account, and a single stock/ETF should not exceed **30% overnight**.

```text
shares = account_risk_dollars / (entry_price - stop_price)
position_value = shares * entry_price
```

If the formula produces a position that is too large for the account cap or the stock's liquidity, reduce size or skip the trade.

## Key Principles

- Position size is determined by **stop distance + max risk**, not gut feel
- Trim 1/3 at **2R** (twice initial risk) to lock in early profits (Alex's approach)
- Never add to a losing trade; only add when the stock is proving you right
- Cushion (unrealized gains) earned early is the best form of leverage
- Qullamaggie-style breakouts and EPs need low-of-day stops tight enough that position size can stay within account and liquidity limits

# Qullamaggie Breakouts and Episodic Pivots

**Summary**: Kristjan Kullamagi (@Qullamaggie) is a momentum swing trader whose edge is built around a small number of repeatable fast-moving stock patterns: common breakouts, episodic pivots, and parabolic short/long setups. For Jie Sheng's scoring system, the most important long-side frameworks are breakouts and episodic pivots.

**Sources**: Qullamaggie official "3 timeless setups" post, Qullamaggie official "How to master Episodic Pivots" post, Qullamaggie FAQ, ChartMill episodic pivot screening notes.

**Last updated**: 2026-05-06

## Core Philosophy

Qullamaggie is not a general-purpose pattern trader. The system is about mastering a few high-velocity setups that have appeared repeatedly across decades and markets.

His main long-side edge:

1. Find stocks with exceptional recent momentum or a fresh catalyst.
2. Wait for a clean risk point instead of chasing randomly.
3. Enter through opening-range highs or daily breakout levels.
4. Define risk immediately, usually at the low of day.
5. Take partial profits quickly enough to protect the trade, then let the remainder trend with moving averages.

The goal is not a high win rate. The goal is small controlled losses plus occasional 5R, 10R, or larger winners.

---

## Setup 1: Common Breakout

### Preconditions

The stock should already be a leader.

| Requirement | Rule |
|---|---|
| Recent momentum | Top 1-2% of stocks over 1-month, 3-month, and 6-month windows |
| Prior move | A strong move higher in the prior 1-3 months, often 30-100%+ |
| Base length | Usually 2 weeks to 2 months |
| Base quality | Orderly pullback or sideways consolidation, higher lows, tightening range |
| Moving averages | Price often rides the rising 10-day and 20-day MAs, sometimes 50-day |

### Entry

Enter when price breaks out of the consolidation.

Preferred triggers:

- High of the first 1-minute candle.
- High of the first 5-minute candle.
- High of the first 60-minute candle.
- Clean daily breakout above the base if intraday precision is not used.

Anticipating the breakout is possible but lower quality and requires more experience.

### Stop

Initial stop is the low of day. The stop should not be wider than ATR or ADR; if the stop is wider than the stock's normal daily range, risk/reward is usually broken.

### Exit

Qullamaggie's default breakout management:

1. Sell 1/3 to 1/2 of the position after 3-5 days if the trade works.
2. Move the stop to breakeven after the partial.
3. Trail the rest with the 10-day or 20-day moving average.
4. For beginners, use the 10-day MA and wait for the first close below it.

### Breakout Failure Patterns

Penalize a trade when any of these appear:

- No strong prior move.
- Base is loose, wide, or messy.
- Breakout runs directly into overhead supply.
- Range is not tightening before expansion.
- Volume does not confirm the breakout.
- Entry is far above the base or late after the move.
- Stop to low-of-day is wider than ATR/ADR.

## Setup 2: Episodic Pivot

### Definition

An episodic pivot is a catalyst-driven repricing event. The best long EPs usually start with unexpected good news, especially earnings or guidance, that forces institutions to revalue the stock.

### Preconditions

| Requirement | Rule |
|---|---|
| Catalyst | Fresh news, earnings, guidance, major contract, regulatory event, product launch, or similar repricing event |
| Gap | Ideally 10%+ gap up |
| Volume | Huge volume near the open; best cases may trade average daily volume within the first 15-30 minutes |
| Fundamentals for earnings EP | Strong EPS/revenue growth and meaningful beat versus expectations |
| Prior chart | Preferably sideways/neglected before the event, not already extended from a multi-month run |
| Liquidity | Must be tradable at the user's intended size |

ChartMill's practical screen is less strict than Qullamaggie's pure definition, but useful for finding candidates: price above 3, adequate market cap, average volume above 300k, recent gap up, recent earnings catalyst, 5%+ price change, and volume at least 100% above the previous day.

### Entry

Identify the EP in after-hours or pre-market. Enter through opening-range highs:

- 1-minute ORH for the earliest trigger.
- 5-minute ORH for more confirmation.
- 60-minute ORH for slower confirmation.

Avoid EPs that gap straight into major resistance or fail to hold momentum after the opening range.

### Stop

Initial stop is the low of day. This keeps risk defined around the day the market repriced the stock.

### Exit

Trail with the 10-day or 20-day moving average once those averages rise above the initial stop. The specific trailing rule can be tuned by trader preference, but the key is to let genuine EP trends breathe while cutting failed EPs fast.

### EP Failure Patterns

Penalize a trade when any of these appear:

- Gap is small or catalyst is weak.
- Volume is ordinary.
- Stock already had a major 3-6 month run before the gap.
- This is a second or repeated EP in a short period.
- Stock is too illiquid for the intended size.
- Opening-range breakout fails quickly.
- Close is weak or far from the highs when the trade is meant to swing.

## Risk and Position Sizing

| Rule | Guideline |
|---|---|
| Account risk | Usually 0.25-1.0% per trade; rarely more than 1% |
| Small account risk | Can be 0.5-1.5% early on, but only with strict stop discipline |
| Position size | Most positions are 10-20% of account size |
| Single-name overnight cap | Do not hold more than 30% of account overnight in one stock or ETF |
| Stop basis | Low of day for breakouts and EPs; stop should not exceed ATR/ADR |
| Liquidity cap | Reduce size or skip if the stock cannot absorb the position cleanly |

Position sizing formula:

```text
shares = account_risk_dollars / (entry_price - stop_price)
position_value = shares * entry_price
```

Then cap the result by max position size and liquidity.

## Qullamaggie Scoring Rubric

Use the same 10-point trade-review structure:

| Dimension | Range | Qullamaggie interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| Entry Quality | 0-4 | ORH/daily breakout from a clean base or EP; not late; stop-to-LOD within ATR/ADR |
| Risk Management | 0-3 | Risk 0.25-1%, position 10-20%, max 30% overnight, no liquidity mismatch |
| Setup Alignment | 0-3 | True breakout or true EP with strong prior/catalyst context, volume, and clear air |

### High Score

Score 9-10 when:

- Prior move or catalyst is obvious.
- Base/EP is clean.
- Volume confirms.
- Entry is through an appropriate ORH or breakout level.
- Stop is low-of-day and not wider than ATR/ADR.
- Position size respects risk and liquidity.
- There is clear air above or no immediate overhead supply.

### Low Score

Score 0-4 when:

- Trade is a random chase, not one of the setups.
- Stop is undefined or too wide.
- Entry is late after the breakout.
- Breakout occurs into supply.
- EP has weak catalyst/volume.
- Position size ignores account risk or liquidity.

## Chart Annotation Checklist

When annotating a Qullamaggie-style trade, mark:

1. Prior move or catalyst.
2. Base/consolidation box or EP day.
3. Opening-range high trigger.
4. Low-of-day stop.
5. ATR/ADR stop-width warning if relevant.
6. Volume expansion or lack of volume.
7. Overhead supply/resistance.
8. Ideal partial-sell zone after 3-5 days.
9. 10-day/20-day trailing MA exit area.

Failure labels:

- "No clean base"
- "Breakout into supply"
- "No volume expansion"
- "Late entry"
- "Stop wider than ATR/ADR"
- "Weak EP catalyst"
- "Illiquid for size"
- "Second EP / already extended"

# Trade Journal Workflow (Jie Sheng)

**Summary**: An automated 6-step process for analysing, scoring, and journalling each trade against 7 trader styles — running in Google Sheets and Google Docs via Claude Code.

**Sources**: trade-journal-analyser.md

**Last updated**: 2026-04-23

---

## Purpose

After each trade, Jie Sheng runs the **trade-journal-analyser** skill to:
1. Pull the latest trade from his Google Sheet
2. Look up the sector/industry via Finviz
3. Score the trade against 7 trader styles
4. Compute a composite "evolving style" score
5. Write a structured journal entry to his Google Doc
6. Identify the weakest dimension and generate an improvement note

This creates a **feedback loop** — every trade teaches something, and the pattern over many trades reveals where to improve.

## The 6-Step Process

### Step 0: Confirm Date
Confirm current date in **Malaysia Time (UTC+8)** before proceeding.

### Step 1: Read Latest Trade
Pull the most recent row from Google Sheets with the fields above.

### Step 2: Sector Lookup
Fetch Finviz page for the ticker: `finviz.com/quote.ashx?t=[TICKER]`

Extract:
- Sector
- Industry
- Market Cap
- Country (must be US)

Check/correct sector in the sheet.

### Step 3: Score Against 7 Trader Styles

**Scoring framework (0–10 per trader):**
- Entry Quality: 0–4
- Risk Management: 0–3
- Setup Alignment: 0–3

| Trader | Style Focus |
|---|---|
| @markminervini | SEPA/Superperformance, Stage 2, VCP, tight base |
| @Clement_Ang17 | Swing+Superperformance, liquid leaders, pocket pivots |
| @jfsrev | Mechanical, predefined sell rules |
| @TedHZhang | Portfolio Manager/Institutional, high-quality growth |
| @SRxTrades | Technical Swing+Momentum, chart breakouts, volume |
| @PrimeTrading_ | Momentum+Price Action, tight stops, quick exits |
| @Qullamaggie | Momentum Breakouts+EPs, ORH entries, low-of-day stops, ATR/ADR risk guardrails |

See trader-styles for full profiles.

### Step 4: Composite Score
- Calculate weighted average of 6 style scores
- Identify **best-matching trader style**
- Identify **weakest dimension** (Entry / Risk / Setup)
- Generate **evolution note**: one specific improvement for next trade

### Step 5: Journal Entry
Write structured entry to Trade Journal 2026 Google Doc.

**Entry template includes:**
- Date, Ticker, Sector, Industry
- Buy-in price + quantity
- P&L (open or closed)
- Sector note and trade context
- Trader style analysis table (all 7 scores)
- Composite score + best-match style
- Weakest dimension + evolution note
- Entry verdict

### Step 6: Summary to User
Brief summary of analysis and key improvement point.

## Why This Matters

Trading without journaling is trading without feedback. The journal:
- Reveals **behavioral leakages** (see clement-ang-may-2025-recap)
- Tracks which trader style is emerging as Jie Sheng's natural fit
- Creates an accountability record for review during quiet markets
- Computes objective scores rather than subjective "I felt good about that trade"
